## 2. What to continue

- **Squat-forward worked.** Prioritizing low-bar squat on D1 (top + backoff) drove the biggest
  e1RM jump of any lift. The squat clearly rewards being the priority — keep featuring it.
- **Bench-variant rotation kept the shoulder safe** while still adding 23 e1RM on CGB. Spoto and
  Larsen both progressed without a single grind. This is the template for benching around the
  injury.
- **Sumo is genuinely strong** (506 e1RM, matching squat). The paused-RDL feeder work is paying off.
- **Larsen bench RPE discipline improved** — capped at 7.5 vs B1's 9. Proof you *can* hold a cap
  when you decide to.

## 3. What to improve / change

- **Top-end RPE still drifts.** Squat 475 @8.5, CGB 235 @8.5, WPU BW+100 @9. The everyday lifts
  behaved, but the heaviest sets still crept past plan. Same B1 pattern, smaller magnitude —
  improving, not solved.
- **No true deload, then a hard stop.** Skipping W4 and peaking in W5 right before two weeks off
  isn't a deload — it's a peak into a cliff. You got the rest from the trip, but by accident, not
  by design. (This is the open item Block 3 was built to fix.)
- **BW+100 pull-up came at @9.** It's a milestone and worth celebrating, but a @9 single on a lift
  that loads the same structures as your deadlift is a recovery cost. Hit it, log it, don't make
  it a weekly habit.

## Coach's notes — power & health

- **Power:** two 500+ e1RM lifts in one block is the real story — your squat and sumo are
  competition-respectable now. The next gains there come from *consolidation*, not more intensity:
  more quality sets at @7–8, fewer @8.5–9 max-outs. You've proven you can grind to a number; the
  unlock now is patience.
- **Health:** the shoulder got a quiet block (no comp bench, no dips) and tolerated it well — that
  rest is part of why B3's reintroduction is even on the table. The lesson to keep: deliberate
  de-loading of the irritated structures buys you the ability to load them again later. Don't spend
  that credit carelessly in B3.
